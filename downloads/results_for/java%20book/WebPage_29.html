About the Author

Paul J. Deitel, CEO and Chief Technical Officer of Deitel & Associates, Inc., is a graduate of MIT’s Sloan School of Management, where he studied Information Technology. He holds the Java Certified Programmer and Java Certified Developer certifications, and has been designated by Sun Microsystems as a Java Champion. Through Deitel & Associates, Inc., he has delivered Java, C, C++, C# and Visual Basic courses to industry clients, including IBM, Sun Microsystems, Dell, Lucent Technologies, Fidelity, NASA at the Kennedy Space Center, the National Severe Storm Laboratory, White Sands Missile Range, Rogue Wave Software, Boeing, Stratus, Cambridge Technology Partners, Open Environment Corporation, One Wave, Hyperion Software, Adra Systems, Entergy, CableData Systems, Nortel Networks, Puma, iRobot, Invensys and many more. He has also lectured on Java and C++ for the Boston Chapter of the Association for Computing Machinery. He and his father, Dr. Harvey M. Deitel, are the world’s best-selling programming language textbook authors.


Dr. Harvey M. Deitel, Chairman and Chief Strategy Officer of Deitel & Associates, Inc., has 45 years of academic and industry experience in the computer field. Dr. Deitel earned B.S. and M.S. degrees from MIT and a Ph.D. from Boston University. He has 20 years of college teaching experience, including earning tenure and serving as the Chairman of the Computer Science Department at Boston College before founding Deitel & Associates, Inc., with his son, Paul J. Deitel. He and Paul are the co-authors of several dozen books and multimedia packages and they are writing many more. With translations published in Japanese, German, Russian, Spanish, Traditional Chinese, Simplified Chinese, Korean, French, Polish, Italian, Portuguese, Greek, Urdu and Turkish, the Deitels’ texts have earned international recognition. Dr. Deitel has delivered hundreds of professional seminars to major corporations, academic institutions, government organizations and the military.

Jeffrey K. Smith
1.0 out of 5 starsAs bad a programming book as I've ever seen
January 22, 2012
Format: Paperback|Verified Purchase
I am using this book for an Advanced Java class, which means that it is my second semester of Java. I am an experienced software developer who has programmed extensively in C, C++, FORTRAN, IDL, Pascal, Perl, and Python. Fortunately, when I first learned to program, the instructors had us use books that were much better than this one. This book is simply abysmal. When I read the reviews that gave this book a good rating, I notice 2 things:
1) Most of them appear to have only read the first few chapters;
2) Few of them have significant work experience as software developers.

This is yet another programming book that just throws out a bunch of examples rather than presenting explanations. Apparently, the authors realize that there is a large market segment of unsophisticated people who think that if they type in example programs then they are learning a programming language, and have targeted that market.

The discussions of most of the examples do little but point out the obvious (many are not much more than "line 77 uses the read method of the file object to read the file"). Typing in examples might make unsophisticated programmers feel like they are learning the language, but what happens if there is the need to write code that isn't similar to any of the examples? You end up wasting lots of time programming by trial-and-error.

Generally only those aspects of a topic that are needed to understand the examples are discussed in the book; you have to use another book or search the Internet to find out the rest. In fact, when doing the exercises I google to find the needed information rather than consulting the book, since the book won't necessarily contain the information needed to do the exercises.

To find out how to use any of the Java class libraries, you have to go on to the Internet because this book does not actually discuss the syntax and usage of any of the important Java classes. This makes the book worthless as a reference.

Many of the later chapters (17 and beyond) are completely worthless. I got nothing out of reading them.

All in all, a waste of money. To actually write your own programs in Java, you'll need to buy a book that explains Java rather than that just explains Java example programs. So why not just buy that other book, which is likely to be a lot cheaper than this one? Core Java would be a good alternative.
